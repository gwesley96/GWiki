(Pseudo-)Riemannian Metric

Last modified: 2025-12-11 19:52
Created: 2025-12-09
Tags: differential-geometry

Idea. Metrics capture the quadratic data needed to measure lengths and angles; pseudo-Riemannian metrics relax positivity to accommodate signatures such as Lorentzian geometries while retaining smooth variation over the manifold.

Definition (Pseudo-inner product). A pseudo-inner product (resp. inner product) on a vector space $V$ is a positive-semidefinite form (resp. positive-definite form) symmetric bilinear form $\bkt{-}{-}\colon V\times V\to\bbR$.

Definition ((Pseudo-)Euclidean vector spaces). A pseudo-Euclidean vector space (resp. Euclidean vector space) is a finite-dimensional $\bbR$-vector space $V$ equipped with a positive-semidefinite (resp. positive-definite) quadratic form $Q\colon V\to\bbR$.

Remark ((Pseudo-)norms from quadratic forms). A (pseudo-)Euclidean vector space $(V,Q)$ has (pseudo-)norm $\|-\|\coloneqq \sqrt{Q(-)}$.

(Pseudo-)Riemannian metrics

Definition. A pseudo-Riemannian metric on a smooth manifold $M$ is a $(0,2)$-tensor field $\mathrm{g}$ such that for each $p\in M$, the bilinear form $\mathrm{g}_p\coloneqq \bkt{-}{-}_p\colon T_pM\times T_pM\to \bbR$ is symmetric and nondegenerate. If $\mathrm{g}$ is positive-definite, it is a Riemannian metric.

Definition ((Pseudo-)Riemannian manifolds). A (pseudo-)Riemannian manifold is a smooth manifold $M$ equipped with a (pseudo-)Riemannian metric $\mathrm{g} \coloneqq \bkt{-}{-}$.

Example (Euclidean metric). For $M = \bbR^n$ with global coordinates $x^i$, the Euclidean metric $\overline{\mathrm{g}} \coloneqq (\mathrm{d} x^1)^2 + \cdots + (\mathrm{d} x^n)^2$ makes $\bbR^n$ into a Riemannian manifold, the standard Euclidean vector space of dimension $n$.

Example (Poincaré disk model). For $M = \overset\circ D{}^n \subset \bbR^n$, the Poincaré metric $\mathrm{g}_{\small\mathbb{H}}\coloneqq \dfrac{4\overline{\mathrm{g}}}{1-\overline{\mathrm{g}}}$ yields the Poincaré disk model of $n$-dimensional hyperbolic space $\mathbb{H}^n$.

Proposition ((Lee18, Prop. 2.4)). Every smooth manifold admits a Riemannian metric; one proof uses a Partition of Unity.
